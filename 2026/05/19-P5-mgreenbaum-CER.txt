CLAIM:
The expansion of tuskless elephant populations in Gorongosa National Park exemplifies how environmental bottlenecks combined with non-random survival can rapidly shift genetic composition, providing direct evidence that microevolutionary processes operate at observable timescales when selection pressures are sufficiently intense.

EVIDENCE:
Field surveys document that tuskless elephants comprised approximately 5% of the female population in the 1970s, before reaching over 50% by 2015, while male tusklessness remained below 10%. This dramatic increase follows the Mozambican Civil War period (1977-1992), during which the elephant population crashed from ~60,000 to ~300 individuals due to systematic poaching targeting tusked animals. Genetic studies confirm the tuskless trait is controlled by a dominant allele linked to the X chromosome, creating a sex-biased expression pattern. Post-war population recovery showed tuskless individuals had improved reproductive success and survival rates compared to tusked counterparts, with tuskless females producing more viable offspring and living longer. DNA analysis of population samples revealed that previously rare alleles became common, demonstrating measurable changes in allele frequency across generations.

REASONING:
The observed shift in tusklessness frequency reflects the core mechanism of evolution: differential reproductive success based on heritable traits. The poaching-induced population bottleneck eliminated most tusked individuals, instantly reducing genetic diversity while simultaneously removing the tusked phenotype from the breeding population. Survivors with tuskless genotypes reproduced at higher rates because they avoided poaching and reached reproductive maturity more frequently than tusked individuals. As tuskless elephants contributed disproportionately to the next generation, the frequency of the tusklessness allele increased significantly. The X-linked inheritance pattern accelerated this change in females, who transmit the allele to all offspring. This case demonstrates that evolution by natural selection is not a hypothetical process confined to fossil records but an active mechanism producing measurable phenotypic and genetic change within human observation timescales.
