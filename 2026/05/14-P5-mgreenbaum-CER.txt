CLAIM:
The tuskless phenotype in Gorongosa elephants is inherited as an X-linked dominant trait, explaining the higher prevalence of tusklessness in females compared to males.

EVIDENCE:
Genetic analysis of elephant populations in Gorongosa National Park reveals that tuskless females occur at significantly higher frequencies (over 50% in recent surveys) compared to tuskless males (approximately 7% of adult males). The distribution of the tuskless phenotype across sexes follows a predictable pattern consistent with X-linked inheritance. Female elephants, having two X chromosomes, can express the tuskless phenotype in both heterozygous (carrier) and homozygous states, while males with one X chromosome would only need a single dominant allele to express the trait. Historical records document the progression of this trait through the elephant population following the Mozambican Civil War, with genetic markers confirming the presence of a specific allele responsible for tusklessness.

REASONING:
If the tuskless allele is dominant and located on the X chromosome, heterozygous females (Xx) and homozygous dominant females (XX) would both display the tuskless phenotype, while males would only need one copy of the allele (X^T Y) to be tuskless. This explains why the proportion of tuskless females far exceeds that of tuskless males in the population. The pattern observed in Gorongosa—high female tusklessness and low male tusklessness—matches predictions for an X-linked dominant inheritance pattern. This genetic mechanism reveals how a single allele can rapidly spread through a population under intense selective pressure, as males carrying the allele would contribute it to all daughters while passing their Y chromosome to sons, creating an asymmetrical inheritance pattern that accelerates the trait's increase in females.
