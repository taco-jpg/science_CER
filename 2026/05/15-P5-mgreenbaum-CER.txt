CLAIM:
The rapid increase in tusklessness among Gorongosa elephants demonstrates how artificial selection through poaching acts as a powerful evolutionary force, fundamentally reshaping the genetic composition of a wild population within a single generation.

EVIDENCE:
In Gorongosa National Park, the frequency of tuskless females increased from nearly 0% in the 1970s to over 50% in current populations, representing a dramatic shift in allele frequency following the Mozambican Civil War (1977-1992). During this period, intensive poaching targeted tusked elephants for ivory trade, effectively removing individuals carrying the tusked allele from the breeding population at higher rates than tuskless individuals. Genetic analysis confirms that the tuskless phenotype is controlled by a dominant allele located on the X chromosome, which explains the sex-specific distribution: females comprise over 50% of tuskless elephants while males represent only approximately 7% of the tuskless population. This pattern is consistent with the expected inheritance ratio for X-linked traits in sexually reproducing populations.

REASONING:
The tuskless allele increased in frequency because tusked elephants faced severe predation pressure from human poaching, creating a situation of artificial selection analogous to natural selection. Since tuskless individuals had higher survival rates and therefore greater reproductive success, they passed the tuskless allele to offspring at proportionally higher frequencies than tusked individuals. The X-linked nature of the trait explains why females show disproportionately higher tusklessness rates: heterozygous females (Xx) express the dominant tuskless phenotype and contribute the allele to all daughters, while males can only pass their allele to daughters through X-chromosome inheritance. This case study illustrates how human activity can create selective pressures strong enough to drive observable evolution in wild populations within two to three decades, challenging the traditional view of evolution as an exclusively slow process operating over geological timescales.
