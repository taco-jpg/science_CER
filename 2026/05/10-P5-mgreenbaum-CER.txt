CLAIM:
The prevalence of tuskless elephants in Gorongosa National Park has increased dramatically due to strong selective pressure from poaching during the Mozambican Civil War.

EVIDENCE:
Before the civil war in 1977, tuskless elephants were extremely rare in Gorongosa National Park, representing less than 1% of the female population. During and after the war (1977-1992), intense ivory poaching created a survival disadvantage for tusked elephants. Genetic analysis reveals that tusklessness is controlled by a dominant allele on the X chromosome, meaning heterozygous females can express the trait. Post-war surveys show that tuskless females now comprise over 50% of the population, while tuskless males remain at approximately 7%. This sex-biased ratio is consistent with X-linked inheritance, where males need only one copy of the recessive allele to be tuskless, while females are more likely to be heterozygous (tusked) carriers or homozygous tuskless individuals.

REASONING:
This rapid change in allele frequency over a 25-30 year period demonstrates natural selection acting on the elephant population in real time. Poachers preferentially hunted tusked elephants for ivory, directly reducing their reproductive success and eliminating them from the breeding population. Tuskless elephants, being less profitable targets, survived poaching at higher rates and reproduced successfully, increasing the frequency of the tusklessness allele in subsequent generations. The consistent pattern of X-linked inheritance across the population confirms that this is not simply environmental variation but a heritable genetic change. This example provides compelling evidence that evolution operates on rapid timescales when selective pressures are sufficiently strong, illustrating how human activities can drive measurable evolutionary change within single generations.
