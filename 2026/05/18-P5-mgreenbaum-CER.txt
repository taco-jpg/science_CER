CLAIM:
The prevalence of tuskless elephants in Gorongosa National Park represents a case of rapid adaptive evolution driven by intense human-mediated selection pressure, demonstrating that populations can evolve measurable phenotypic changes within decades rather than geological timescales.

EVIDENCE:
Between the 1970s and the present day, the proportion of tuskless female elephants in Gorongosa National Park increased from less than 5% to over 50%, while tuskless males remained around 7% of the adult population. This shift occurred during and immediately following the Mozambican Civil War (1977-1992), when organized poaching eliminated the vast majority of tusked elephants to supply the ivory trade. Genetic analysis reveals that the tuskless phenotype is controlled by a dominant allele on the X chromosome, explaining the sex-biased distribution where heterozygous females (Xx) and hemizygous males (X^+Y) express tusklessness. Field surveys and historical documentation confirm that these changes occurred within a single human generation, with reproductive data showing higher survival and breeding success in tuskless individuals compared to tusked counterparts during the post-war period.

REASONING:
This rapid shift in allele frequency satisfies the fundamental definition of evolution: a change in allele frequencies within a population over time. The selective removal of tusked elephants through poaching created an artificial selection pressure strong enough to drive visible evolutionary change. Individuals carrying the tuskless allele had disproportionately higher fitness (survival and reproduction) because tusks made them targets for hunting, while tuskless individuals survived to reproductive maturity and contributed their genes to the next generation at higher frequencies. The X-linked inheritance pattern means female carriers transmit the allele to all daughters, further accelerating its spread through the population. This case demonstrates that evolution is not confined to slow processes operating over millions of years; rather, when selection pressures are sufficiently intense, populations can exhibit measurable adaptive change within timescales observable by human researchers.
